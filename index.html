Introduction

In rhetoric and writing studies, much of the scholarship about podcasting is pedagogical scholarship. Researchers have investigated using podcasts as teaching tools to develop multimodal listening habits (Ceraso, 2018), to highlight embodied writing practices (Cushman & Kelly, 2018), to rethink ancient rhetorical exercises (Detweiler, 2019), to affect social change (Choong & Bjork, 2023), to augment students’ rhetorical awareness (Klein, 2020), and to teach students to compose rhetorically with sound (Danforth & Stedman, 2018). Yet most of this research on podcasting pedagogy focuses on face–to–face teaching, not online learning.

At the same time, the rapid development of online writing instruction (OWI) scholarship has provided a wealth of insights to online teachers (Warnock, 2009; Hewett & Depew, 2015; Han, Gunn, & Chidlow, 2017; Rice & Amant, 2018; Bjork, 2018; Borgman & McCardle, 2019; Hewett, Bourelle, & Warnock, 2021; Rodrigo & Mitchum, 2024; Mahaffey & Walden, 2025). Among these books, Multimedia in the College Classroom (Harris & Greer, 2024) has stood out for its emphasis on incorporating audio and video in both synchronous and asynchronous online writing courses. But OWI research has yet to fully consider podcasting as a rhetorical tool for online pedagogies.

We take inspiration from these two threads of scholarship—podcast pedagogies and OWI—to ask: how might a pedagogical podcast contribute to online rhetoric and writing instruction? We know that instructors in rhetoric and writing studies have long been using podcast episodes in their pedagogies. But we also know that it is less common to create a new podcast for a specific course, especially when so many instructors in our discipline teach in limited and precarious positions. Still, with the proliferation of online teaching since the Covid–19 pandemic and the continued growth of the podcasting medium, now is the time to investigate what a dedicated pedagogical podcast can offer to multimodal online writing instruction.

This webtext reflects on nearly four years of producing and teaching a pedagogical podcast called Pod Uni for an online podcasting course in Aotearoa New Zealand. Rhetoric, podcasting, and teaching are all dialogic forms of engagement, so in the remainder of this webtext we offer a series of dialogic reflections on our online podcasting pedagogies, presented as short audio recordings or mini podcast episodes. These audio recordings are part of a yet–to–be–released full–length episode of Pod Uni where we provide our listeners—and our students—with a behind–the–scenes look at the rhetorical decisions that go into composing a pedagogical podcast like this. For this webtext, we have selected the most relevant parts of that episode to include here, and for each audio clip, we have highlighted specific takeaways that pertain to using a pedagogical podcast in OWI. We also include in this webtext the first two episodes of Pod Uni (only 2-minutes each) to provide important context about the rhetorical and cultural context of Pod Uni. And of course, we provide a transcript for these audio recordings—as we do in Pod Uni—for accessibility.

As a compilation of short podcast episodes and audio compositions, this webtext also extends a larger transdisciplinary conversation about podcasting as scholarship. Researchers in rhetoric and writing studies have long challenged normative assumptions about the form, shape, and sound of scholarship. Kairos has a long line of webtexts that illustrate the different possibilities of audio–as–scholarship: Janine Butler (2016), Crystal VanKooten (2016), José Manuel Flores and Lucía Durá (2021), Karrieann Soto Vega and Steven Hammer (2021), A.D. Carson (2021), Jonathon W. Stone (2021), Hannah McGregor and Stacey Copeland (2022), and Kyle D. Stedman (2024).Enculturation has also played an important role in laying the groundwork for audio–as–scholarship through its Sonic Projects Archive and Intermezzo texts such Tuning in to Soundwriting (Stedman, Danforth, & Faris, 2021) and Sound Never Tasted So Good (Ceraso, 2019). So too has the Journal of Multimodal Rhetorics (e.g. Hammer & Sieber, 2019; Soto Vega, 2019; Lambke, 2019). Additionally, disciplinary podcasts such as Rhetoricity, Pedagogue, The Big Rhetorical Podcast, Global Rhetorics, Everyone’s Writing with AI (Except Me), Kairoscast, and many others also play an important role in the larger research ecosystem of rhetoric and writing studies. In sum, rhetoric and composition has a rich history of audio scholarship, and this webtext extends this storied scholarly tradition by incorporating a podcast into a webtext that investigates the role of podcasting in online writing pedagogies.

This webtext is thus both an extension of Pod Uni and a critical inquiry into its potential for rhetorical pedagogies in online environments. From our perspective, Pod Uni functions as proof–of–concept that, while not a silver bullet, illustrates how instructor–created podcasting can complement existing pedagogical strategies in online rhetoric and writing courses.

Context

In 2021, Collin Bjork launched Pod Uni, a pedagogical podcast about podcasting. We use this podcast to teach a podcasting course, which is offered in both face–to–face and online asynchronous modes (although the vast majority of students take the online version). This podcasting course functions as a digital writing and rhetoric course where students learn rhetorical techniques and audio writing strategies to compose a single narrative nonfiction, or audio documentary, episode of a podcast. The publicly available Pod Uni podcast is also informed by rhetorical principles, although, as Dr Bjork explains later, the rhetorical elements of the podcast are often disguised with the hope that the podcast may have a wider disciplinary reach beyond rhetoric and writing courses.

We teach at Te Kunenga ki Pūrehuroa Massey University in Aotearoa New Zealand. Our university has been engaged in distance education for over sixty years and online education for over twenty years. In the past several years, the majority of our humanities students have been online students. Most of Aotearoa’s universities are located in the country’s larger and more expensive cities, which can be prohibitively expensive for many students. Our online courses make a university education accessible to students who live outside of these urban centers as well as to students who work full–time and/or have caretaking duties. To accommodate students’ busy work and family commitments, our online podcasting course, like many others at our institution, is primarily asynchronous. We offer a handful of optional Zoom sessions that are only lightly attended, but otherwise our course is fully delivered through the learning management system and the Pod Uni podcast.

For more specific context about this podcast, listen to the first two episodes below. Each episode is only two minutes long. The second episode features a karakia, or Māori chant, that pertains to the multimodal writing.

We begin with a karakia, with a chant, from Māori culture about "tuhia," which is related to the word "tuhituhi" (to write). But this notion of tuhia or tuhituhi predates European notions of writing. Indeed, tuhituhi might be more like something like to inscribe or to make one's mark on the world or to make one's mark, as the karakia says, on the sky, the land, and the hearts of the people. Indeed, podcasting is this opportunity to mark the world and the hearts of others through our kōrero, through our speech, and indeed to be marked in return through the kōrero of others. And indeed, then, we can haumi e, hui e taiki e, we can bind it and stitch it together; stitch together our communities, our dialogue, ourselves, our world. Takeaways

The following four takeaways express some of the most important things we've learned in our experimentation with incorporating an instructor-created podcast into an online multimodal writing course. Takeaway 1

A pedagogical podcast is a medium–appropriate way to meet the needs of online students in a multimodal composition course.

Pros & Cons

When designing a pedagogical podcast for OWI, we argue that a pedagogical podcast can complement and amplify existing online writing pedagogies, but like any pedagogical tool, it’s not without its downsides. In the following audio clip, Dr. Gunn discusses the benefits and drawbacks of both producing and teaching with a pedagogical podcast in OWI.

Benefits:

  • A pedagogical podcast is a helpful resource for online students, especially ones who have lots of time constraints (e.g. parents, full–time workers, and caretakers)
  • A pedagogical podcast helps instructors empathise with students engaged in multimodal composition
  • A pedagogical podcast is a valuable multidisciplinary and multimodal activity for instructors’ own professional development

Drawbacks:

  • Not all students listen to the pedagogical podcast, including the students who may need it most (but this is a perennial problem in teaching)
  • It’s a LOT of work to produce and promote a pedagogical podcast to a high standard
  • It would be more effective if a pedagogical podcast not only provided meta–commentary about multimodal composition but also provided examples of multimodal composition, including examples composed by students

Conclusion

As we outline above, when teaching multimodal composition online, a pedagogical podcast can complement existing pedagogies by providing students with an opportunity to engage course content on their own time in a medium that aligns with both their multimodal writing goals and with their needs as online students. A pedagogical podcast also provides an opportunity to showcase examples of multimodal composition and even prior students’ work. And as a public–facing medium, a pedagogical podcast can help connect university communities with larger public interest groups. But a pedagogical podcast does require considerable additional work for instructors who may already work in precarious contexts. And as with print textbooks, there’s no guarantee that students will consistently engage with a pedagogical podcast.

Still, we contend that a pedagogical podcast is—especially in an audio composition course—one productive way to engage online students and to complement existing pedagogical strategies. And although podcasts can sometimes appear to exist in an amorphous digital ether that is detached from a local context, we found that it was enriching for both our students and for our wider listenership to ground our podcast in our immediate cultural context here on Māori whenua in Aotearoa New Zealand. We encourage other instructors who create a pedagogical podcast to do the same. We are certain that there is much more to be learned about pedagogical podcasting in both online and face–to–face teaching in rhetoric and composition, and we look forward to hearing what future teacher–researchers accomplish by including pedagogical podcasts in their own courses.
