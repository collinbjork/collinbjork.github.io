Introduction

In rhetoric and writing studies, much of the scholarship about podcasting is pedagogical scholarship. Researchers have investigated using podcasts as teaching tools to expand the five canons of rhetoric (Jennifer Bowie, 2012), to develop multimodal listening habits (Steph Ceraso, 2018), to highlight embodied writing practices (Jeremy Cushman & Shannon Kelly, 2018), to rethink ancient rhetorical exercises (Eric Detweiler, 2019), to affect social change (Philip Choong & Collin Bjork, 2023), and to augment students’ rhetorical awareness (Alison Klein, 2020). This scholarship emerged from a much longer disciplinary tradition of research about the intersection between sound and writing. A 2006 special issue of Computers and Compositioncite> titled “Sound in/as Composition Space,” co–edited by Cheryl Ball and Byron Hawk, illustrated how teacher–scholars interested in digital writing and rhetoric have been focused investigated “other modes of meaning including audio (p. 263) for over two decades. That collection included studies of hip–hop sampling as audio composition (Mickey Hess, 2006), non–diegetic music in film (Bump Halbritter, 2006), and the sonic elements of poetic webtexts (Heidi McKee, 2006). Early research like this established soundwriting as an important site for rhetorical inquiry, and it didn’t take long for a wider swathe of teacher–scholars to begin experimenting with using sound in the composition classroom.

In a seminal text about soundwriting, Cynthia Selfe (2009) summarized key moments in the history of aural composing and examined its uptake, or lack thereof, in 150 years of American university–level writing instruction. She concluded that the field’s emphasis on alphabetic composition limits students’ rhetorical resources for meaning making and, by contrast, that by including aurality in composition instruction, “we expand the field of play for students” (p. 644). Among the many teacher–scholars who answered Selfe’s call to take up more multimodal composition with an emphasis on sound, Kyle D. Stedman’s edited collections with Courtney S. Danforth and Michael J. Faris stand out for the breadth, depth, and diversity of their contributions (Danforth, Stedman, & Faris, 2018; Stedman, Danforth, & Faris, 2021; Faris, Danforth, & Stedman, 2022). These collections explicitly connect to theories and practices of composition pedagogy to sonic rhetorics and offer writing teachers an inspiring array of teaching strategies for navigating soundwriting and deafness (Jennifer J. Buckner & Kirsten Daley, 2018), sound writing as critical resistance to racial oppression (Michael Burns, et al., 2018), sound mixing as rhetorical practice (Mathew Gomes, 2021), writing with hip–hop music (Todd Craig, 2022), and oral histories as composition (Janice W. Fernheimer, 2022). They also intersect with recent research in Kairos about the Canadian mixtape as soundwriting pedagogy (Rich Shivener, et al., 2021) and “extracurricular soundwriting pedagogy” aimed at institutional change (Jennifer Sano–Franchini, et al., 2021). Yet despite the richness of the research on soundwriting and podcasting pedagogies, much of it focuses on face–to–face teaching, not online learning.

At the same time, the rapid development of online writing instruction (OWI) scholarship has provided a wealth of insights to online teachers (Scott J. Warnock, 2009; Beth L. Hewett & Kevin Eric Depew, 2015a; Dahlia Han, Melissa Gunn, & Rachel Chidlow, 2017; Rich Rice & Kirk St. Amant, 2018; Collin Bjork, 2018; Jesse Borgman & Casey McArdle, 2019; Beth L. Hewett, Tiffany Bourelle, & Scott J. Warnock, 2021; Rochelle Rodrigo & Catrina Mitchum, 2024; Cat Mahaffey & Ashlyn Walden, 2025). Like podcasting pedagogy research, today’s online writing instruction scholarship builds on a long line scholarship about teaching composition, including multimodal composition, in mediated and online environments. After all, the very first issue of Kairos examined online writing labs (OWLs), asking how this form of pedagogy impacts ESL students (Suzan Moody, 1996), literacy (J. Paul Johnson, 1996), and writing instruction writ large (Stuart Blythe, 1996). A decade later, a special issue of Computers and Composition titled “Distance Learning: Evolving Perspectives,” edited by Jane Blakelock and Tracy E. Smith (2006), augmented this field and included some articles that paid special attention to the politics of online writing instruction (Kristine Blair & Cheryl Hoy, 2006; Colleen A. Reilly & Joseph John Williams, 2006). Today, the OWI Community, the Global Society of Online Literacy Educators (GSOLE), the members of the CCCC OWI Committee, and hundreds of instructors around the globe have built a substantial body of collective knowledge about online writing instruction.

Among this scholarship, Multimedia in the College Classroom by Heidi Skurat Harris & Michael Greer (2024) stands out for highlighting the importance of incorporating multimedia in OWI. They explained that “[t]he assumption that online course design is text–driven is so prevalent that it is almost invisible” (p. 9). Their research drew heavily on educational psychologist Richard E. Mayer who primarily understands multimedia as communication that contains both words and images. As such, their book emphasized the visual elements of online writing instruction more than the sonic dimensions of teaching composition online. This focus on the textual, and now the visual, elements of online writing instruction illuminates how the field has yet to fully explore its connections with sonic rhetorics and soundwriting pedagogies. Conversely, the sub–discipline of soundwriting pedagogies can augment its body of research by further investigating how its practices function in the context of hybrid and online learning environments. This webtext aims to link and extend these bodies of scholarship by asking: how might a pedagogical podcast contribute to online writing instruction?

First, some definitions. In this webtext, we follow the CCCC OWI Committee in defining online writing instruction as:

writing instruction that occurs—at least partially if not fully—in a computer–based, Internet, or intranet instructional setting. It uses online/digital media to provide instruction; to talk about writing; or to distribute, share, and/or collect writing–related materials. OWI can occur in either the synchronous or asynchronous modality using a variety of electronic media, platforms, and technologies. (Hewett and Depew, 2015b, p. 2)

This definition is, as Hewett and Depew reiterated, “inclusive of both the hybrid and the fully online settings” (2015b, p. 10). Such a fluid definition of online writing instruction suits our own institutional context in which we simultaneously teach both hybrid and fully–online versions of our podcasting course, which is taught through the lens of multimodal composition.

We also need to define a pedagogical podcast and identify the points of convergence and divergence between pedagogical podcasts and their near cousin, educational podcasts. In his taxonomy of podcast genres, Eric Detweiler (2021) defines educational podcasts as a genre that “typically revolves around one person, or occasionally more than one, offering information or instruction to listeners.” Pedagogical podcasts, in our view, are a kind of educational podcast that focuses not just on broadly informing or instructing listeners but also, like a textbook, operates as an explicit part of the course design. While many podcasts are educational, a podcast becomes explicitly pedagogical when instructors incorporate it into a carefully designed set of instructional materials. As with many podcast genres, the lines between educational and pedagogical podcasts are fluid. For instance, an educational podcast such as Ted Talks Daily may become a pedagogical podcast when an instructor assigns an episode in one of their courses. Conversely, a pedagogical podcast that is created specifically as a classroom resource like a textbook, becomes part of the larger collection of educational podcasts when wider publics—beyond the students in course it is designed for—listen to and learn from its episodes. Pedagogical podcasts can be used in online, hybrid, or face–to–face instructional environments.

We know that instructors in rhetoric and writing studies have long been using podcast episodes in their pedagogies. But we also know that it is less common to create a new podcast for a specific course, especially when so many instructors in our discipline teach in limited and precarious positions. So we decided to create a pedagogical podcast and to evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of incorporating it into our hybrid and online podcasting course. This webtext reflects on nearly four years of producing and teaching a pedagogical podcast called Pod Uni for use in online and hybrid versions of a podcasting course at a large public university in Aotearoa New Zealand. Based on our initial observations, we argue that a pedagogical podcast can bolster existing online writing instruction pedagogies especially when teaching multimodal composition, but that creating a pedagogical podcast also places notable—and in some cases untenable—demands on online writing instructors’ time, labor, and privacy.

Context

In 2021, Collin Bjork launched Pod Uni, an audio–only pedagogical podcast about podcasting. We use this podcast to teach a podcasting course, which is offered in both hybrid and online asynchronous modes (although the vast majority of students take the online version). This podcasting course functions as a digital writing and rhetoric course where students learn rhetorical techniques and audio writing strategies to compose a single narrative nonfiction, or audio documentary, episode of a podcast. The publicly available Pod Uni podcast is also informed by rhetorical principles, although, as Dr. Bjork explains later, the rhetorical elements of the podcast are often disguised with the hope that the podcast may have a wider disciplinary reach beyond rhetoric and writing courses.

We teach at Te Kunenga ki Pūrehuroa Massey University in Aotearoa New Zealand. Our university has been engaged in distance education for over sixty years and online education for over twenty years. In the past several years, the majority of our humanities students have been online students. Most of Aotearoa’s universities are located in the country’s larger and more expensive cities, which can be prohibitively expensive for many students. Our online courses make a university education accessible to students who live outside of these urban centers as well as to students who work full–time, have caretaking duties, and/or have disabilities that make face–to–face courses extra challenging. To accommodate students’ busy work and family commitments, our online podcasting course, like many others at our institution, is primarily asynchronous. We offer a handful of optional Zoom sessions that are only lightly attended, but otherwise our course is fully delivered through the learning management system and the Pod Uni podcast. The hybrid version of this same course requires students to do a few hours of independent work, including listening to episodes of the Pod Uni podcast, prior to a two–hour tutorial session offered once a week. While it is possible that a pedagogical podcast could also work in face–to–face composition courses, we have only tested its use in hybrid and fully online courses, which is our focus here.

The students who take our rhetorically–informed podcasting course in both online and hybrid versions are relatively diverse. The hybrid students tend to be younger, most just out of high school; while the fully online students are on average several years older, often working professionals and young parents. In terms of race, both hybrid and online cohorts tend to roughly mirror racial diversity of the larger nation, which is to say predominantly white (or “New Zealand European,” as the government terms it) with sizable minorities of Asian, Māori, and Pacific Islander students. Typically very few international students take our course. Each year, we have online students who take our course from Australia and sometimes from other nations too. But these overseas students are all New Zealanders who are working overseas and who choose to study online at Massey University because they get cheaper tuition—what Americans would call “in–state” tuition—than they would if they paid international student rates to study in the country where they live. Thus, while our online students sometimes take our course from a variety of different worldwide locales, those students usually identify as Kiwis and represent roughly the same racial diversity as the domestic students. Given this similarity in our overseas and domestic student cohorts, we typically have fewer “friction points” (Kirk St. Amant, 2013) when teaching our overseas students than other online instructors who teach students across more marked cultural and national boundaries. For us, the primary friction point that our overseas students face is what Rich Rice and Kirk St. Amant called “connecting” in their “3Cs of writing in global online contexts” (2018, p. 8). Because our overseas students typically work full–time and live in different time zones than New Zealand Standard Time, we sometimes struggle to connect “in ways that build a continually interacting community around a shared area of interest” (p. 8). As we discuss in the subsequent sections, the Pod Uni podcast does not fully resolve this friction point, but it does offer another opportunity for connection to our overseas and online students.

In our online and hybrid podcasting courses, we use the Pod Uni podcast as a kind of audio textbook. In a typical week, we assign for homework one episode from Pod Uni that pertains to that week’s focus. As with textbook readings or instructional videos, students are asked to reflect on and/or take notes on the episode and then to apply their learnings in a series of exercises or activities. In the hybrid version of the course, these activities are done collectively during the two–hour class session. In the asynchronous online version of the course, students complete these exercises independently and submit their work online, sometimes for collective discussion and sometimes for private feedback from the instructor.

This webtext does not, however, discuss the specific learning activities or assignments that students complete during the course. Instead, this webtext focuses on the broad implications of what we learned from designing and deploying the Pod Uni podcast in our online and hybrid course. Thus, unlike many other valuable PraxisWiki articles in Kairos, this webtext does not analyze the nuances of syllabus design, specific assignments, or examples of student work because we found that the consequences of creating a pedagogical podcast for OWI reached well beyond individual learning activities and touched on some of the most pressing concerns in our discipline, including the growth of online learning, contingent labor issues, and our collective efforts to refine rhetorically sound digital pedagogies.

Podcasting–as–Scholarship

Rhetoric, podcasting, and teaching are all dialogic forms of engagement, so in the remainder of this webtext we offer a series of dialogic reflections on our online podcasting pedagogies, presented as short audio recordings or mini podcast episodes. These audio recordings are part of a yet–to–be–released full–length episode of Pod Uni where we provide our students, and the wider public, with a behind–the–scenes look at the rhetorical decisions that go into composing a pedagogical podcast like this. We created this episode as a meta–reflection that models and makes visible—for both our students and for other educators who listen to the podcast—the rhetorical decision–making that went into crafting a pedagogical podcast. For this webtext, we have selected the most relevant parts of that episode to include here, and for each audio clip, we have highlighted specific takeaways that pertain to using a pedagogical podcast in OWI. We also include in this webtext the first two episodes of Pod Uni (only 2–minutes each) to provide important context for Kairos readers about the rhetorical and cultural context of Pod Uni. And of course, we provide a transcript for these audio recordings—as we do in Pod Uni—for accessibility.

As a compilation of short podcast episodes and audio compositions, this webtext also extends a larger transdisciplinary conversation about podcasting as scholarship. Researchers in rhetoric and writing studies have long challenged normative assumptions about the form, shape, and sound of scholarship. Kairos has produced a long line of webtexts that illustrate the different possibilities of audio–as–scholarship: Janine Butler (2016), Crystal VanKooten (2016), José Manuel Flores and Lucía Durá (2021), Karrieann Soto Vega and Steven Hammer (2021), Jonathon W. Stone (2021), A.D. Carson (2021), Hannah McGregor and Stacey Copeland (2022), and Kyle D. Stedman (2024). Enculturation has also played an important role in laying the groundwork for audio–as–scholarship through its Sonic Projects Archive and Intermezzo texts such Tuning in to Soundwriting (Kyle D. Stedman, Courtney Danforth, & Michael J. Faris, 2021) and Sound Never Tasted So Good (Steph Ceraso, 2019). So too has the Journal of Multimodal Rhetorics (e.g. Steven Hammer & Greg Sieber, 2019; Karrieann Soto Vega, 2019; Abigail Lambke, 2019). Additionally, disciplinary podcasts such as Rhetoricity, Pedagogue, The Big Rhetorical Podcast, Global Rhetorics, Everyone’s Writing with AI (Except Me), Kairoscast, and many others also have played an important role in the larger research ecosystem of rhetoric and writing studies. In sum, rhetoric and composition has a rich history of audio scholarship, and this webtext extends this storied scholarly tradition by incorporating a podcast into a webtext that investigates the role of podcasting in online writing pedagogies. This webtext is thus both an extension of Pod Uni and a critical inquiry into its potential for rhetorical pedagogies in online environments.

We begin with a karakia, with a chant, from Māori culture about "tuhia," which is related to the word "tuhituhi" (to write). But this notion of tuhia or tuhituhi predates European notions of writing. Indeed, tuhituhi might be more like something like to inscribe or to make one's mark on the world or to make one's mark, as the karakia says, on the sky, the land, and the hearts of the people. Indeed, podcasting is this opportunity to mark the world and the hearts of others through our kōrero, through our speech, and indeed to be marked in return through the kōrero of others. This is what the podcasting and the karakia attunes our energies to. The next part of the karakia says ko te mea nui, ko te aroha: "the most important thing is love," (the aroha). Now it might sound wild to western ears to think of podcasting as aroha, as love, but indeed, to show compassion, to listen carefully with empathy—not an interrogation, not an extraction, but a careful listening, a true dialogue—this is the opportunity to show aroha, to show love. And indeed, then, we can haumi e, hui e taiki e, we can bind it and stitch it together; stitch together our communities, our dialogue, ourselves, our world. This is what the karakia orients ourselves to. This is why we open a podcast about podcasting with "tuhia ki te rangi," the karakia. Thanks for listening! Kia ora.

Takeaways

This section uses a combination of audio clips and textual discussion to describe four major takeaways that emerged from creating this pedagogical podcast and including it in our online pedagogy. We synthesize and reflect on these takeaways as a whole in the final section of this webtext.

Takeaway 1

A pedagogical podcast is a medium–appropriate way to meet some of the needs of online students in a multimodal composition course.

I'm imagining what kind of questions do my listeners want to know? Some of them are students. Some of them are not university students. And what are they likely to want to know out of my interviews? And the second season is a bunch of interviews with podcasting experts or storytelling experts from around Aotearoa New Zealand.

Takeaway 2

Design for multiple audiences from the start because pedagogical podcasts are both student–facing and public–facing.

Pros & Cons

When designing a pedagogical podcast for OWI, we argue that a pedagogical podcast can complement and amplify existing online writing pedagogies, but like any pedagogical tool, it’s not without its downsides. In the following audio clip, Dr. Gunn discusses some of the benefits and drawbacks of both producing and teaching with a pedagogical podcast in OWI. In the concluding section, we connect some of these pros and cons to existing scholarship and expand on them.

Benefits:

  • A pedagogical podcast is a helpful resource for online students, especially ones who have lots of time constraints (e.g. parents, full–time workers, and caretakers).
  • A pedagogical podcast helps instructors empathise with students engaged in multimodal composition.
  • A pedagogical podcast is a valuable multidisciplinary and multimodal activity for instructors’ own professional development.

Drawbacks:

  • It is a mountain of work to produce and promote a pedagogical podcast to a high standard, which is especially difficult for contingent faculty in precarious positions.
  • Not all students listen to the pedagogical podcast, including the students who may need it most (which is a perennial problem in teaching).
  • Producing a public–facing pedagogical podcast presents a privacy risk for instructors.
  • Sharing examples of student podcasts poses privacy risks for students and often requires navigating some institutional bureaucracy.

Conclusion

As we outline above, when teaching multimodal composition online, a pedagogical podcast can complement existing pedagogies by providing students with an opportunity to engage course content on their own time in a medium that aligns with both their multimodal writing goals and with their needs as online students. In this sense, a pedagogical podcast aligns with elements of the “PARS” method of teaching writing online (Borgman & McArdle, 2019). Among the four pillars of this PARS method—personal, accessible, responsive, and strategic—a pedagogical podcast especially amplifies the “personal” (17) and “strategic” (71) elements. A pedagogical podcast enhances the “personal” side of online writing instruction insofar as it functions as a kind of “personal space to engage with your students” (29), one that enacts a user–centered design by helping “establish your ethos with your students” (19). Even the co–production of this webtext—which is a collaboration between a permanent and an adjunct faculty member—can be considered as a kind of “personal administration” (26) in which the development of a personal relationship between a WPA and an instructor augments the overall teaching environment. In terms of the PARS method, a pedagogical podcast is also part of the “strategic design” (72) in an asynchronous online multimodal composition course that asks students to compose with audio. It models for students how to compose with audio, and it allows students to access course materials even without a continuous internet connection, which improves accessibility for online students.

A pedagogical podcast also provides other potential benefits that require further research. For example, our discussion above hypothesizes that a pedagogical podcast could offer an opportunity to showcase examples of multimodal composition and prior students’ work in a medium appropriate–manner. Showcasing student work could potentially amplify both the “personal” and “strategic” elements of the PARS method by providing targeted models for student audio compositions that also establish a sense of connection among students past, present, and future. Furthermore, as a public–facing medium, a pedagogical podcast can potentially help connect university communities with larger public interest groups, which illustrates how a pedagogical podcast is a kind of public writing that has the potential to reach across “town and gown” divides (Accardi, 2017). In this way, a pedagogical podcast deployed in certain instructional contexts could align with community writing initiatives (e.g. Knight, 2022), especially when the podcast emphasizes co–composing with local communities and those student–produced episodes are then shared on the pedagogical podcast. Lastly, we have only tested the use of a pedagogical podcast in hybrid and fully online courses, meaning that it remains up to future researchers to evaluate whether a pedagogical podcast could also prove fruitful in face–to–face composition courses.

At the same time, however, a pedagogical podcast presents some significant downsides to online writing instructors. The biggest of these drawbacks is the sheer amount of extra work it takes to create a high–quality pedagogical podcast. Rarely are online writing instructors given extra time to create teaching materials like a pedagogical podcast that may be seen as supplementary by those who hold the purse strings, which can inhibit other instructors, especially those in precarious positions, from creating a pedagogical podcast. In our case, neither Dr. Bjork nor Dr. Gunn were fully compensated for their labor on this podcast. Dr. Bjork took a leading role in designing and producing the podcast because his labor was partially accounted for through his full-time permanent position. By contrast, Dr. Gunn took a smaller role because her labor on the podcast was not covered by her adjunct teaching contract. We were able to secure a grant to partially account for her work as producer on several episodes, but her contributions as an interviewee on two episodes were unpaid labor. The same goes for this webtext: even though Dr. Gunn has a long track record of publishing peer–reviewed research, she has no such research provision in her teaching contract, so Dr Bjork took a larger role in composing this webtext because conducting research is an explicit part of his role. This difference in our relative contributions to this webtext and to the Pod Uni podcast highlights long–running labor inequities in our discipline (Kahn, Lalicker, & Lynch–Biniek, 2017). It also illustrates how the potential benefits of new teaching tools such as pedagogical podcasts are dis–incentivized by the neoliberal austerity measures that often structure online writing instruction (Welch & Scott, 2016). One additional disadvantage of creating a public–facing podcast is the risk to instructors’ privacy. In our current moment, such privacy concerns are amplified because generative artificial intelligence (AI) increases the ease with which bad actors can take advantage of someone’s voice or likeness, which could result in embarrassment, deep fakes, or doxxing. Generative AI is thus not only a risk to educational equality (Bjork, 2023), it also threatens the teachers who seek to enable equity in education through student–centered pedagogies like this. We chose to make Pod Uni an audio–only podcast, which minimizes the visual privacy risks and also reduces some of the labor associated with producing a podcast. Yet we fully understand that the privacy risks associated with creating a public–facing podcast, especially for those in precarious positions, may not be worth it for some instructors.

A pedagogical podcast is therefore both an opportunity and burden for online writing instructors. In an online audio composition course, a pedagogical podcast is a productive way to engage students and to complement existing pedagogical strategies. And although podcasts can sometimes appear to exist in an amorphous digital ether that is detached from a local context, we found that it was enriching for both our students and for our wider listenership to ground our podcast in our immediate cultural context here on Māori whenua in Aotearoa New Zealand. Yet despite these benefits, we cannot uniformly recommend that other instructors produce similar pedagogical podcasts until the labor issues that have long plagued our discipline and our universities are fully addressed. Indeed, for online writing instructors, the decision whether or not to produce a pedagogical podcast echoes what Professor Sano–Franchini asked at the conclusion of her CCCC Chair’s Address, “How should we continue to make intentional choices about where and how we are going to place our labor, intellectual energy, and personal content?” (14). And given all of the demands on online writing instructors today, a pedagogical podcast may not be a top priority for instructors. Still, we offer this case study of a pedagogical podcasting in online writing instruction as an example of both the structural issues that limit our discipline and the pedagogical potential of investing in innovative methods of teaching multimodal composition online.
